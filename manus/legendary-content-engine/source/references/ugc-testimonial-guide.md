## 1. Testimonial Philosophy

### The Rule
Testimonials should sound like real people, not marketing copy.
The best testimonials describe a specific moment of clarity, not a general
feeling of satisfaction.

### What Makes a Great Testimonial
- Specific (names a moment, not a feeling)
- Surprising (reveals something the reader did not expect)
- Grounded (sounds like a real person talking)
- Transformation-focused (describes what changed, not what was purchased)

### What to Avoid
- Generic praise ("Amazing product! Love it!")
- Superlatives without specificity ("Best thing ever!")
- Testimonials that sound written by marketing
- AI-fabricated quotes (never, under any circumstances)

## 3. Testimonial Editing

### What You Can Edit
- Grammar and spelling
- Length (shorten, never add)
- Clarity (rephrase for readability while preserving meaning)

### What You Cannot Edit
- The core sentiment or meaning
- Specific details or claims
- The emotional tone
- Adding words or ideas the person did not express

### Editing Examples

**Raw**: "omg i literally cant believe how much this changed everything for me like i used to spend SO much money on stuff that looked terrible"

**Edited**: "I cannot believe how much this changed for me. I used to spend so much money on things that looked terrible."

**Too far**: "This product transformed my entire approach to personal style and saved me thousands of dollars." (meaning changed, claims added)

## 4. Client Story Structure

### The Arc

Client stories follow the same four-beat emotional arc as all brand content:

**Beat 1: Before** (Observation)
Name the specific frustration or situation before {{BRAND_NAME}}.

"{{CLIENT_STORY_BEFORE}}"

> Example: "Sarah bought clothes she never wore. Her closet was full, but nothing felt right."

**Beat 2: Discovery** (Realization)
The moment they found {{BRAND_NAME}} and what shifted.

"{{CLIENT_STORY_DISCOVERY}}"

> Example: "When she saw her color palette for the first time, she understood why."

**Beat 3: Change** (Emotional Shift)
The specific, observable change in their behavior or understanding.

"{{CLIENT_STORY_CHANGE}}"

> Example: "Now she buys less, wears everything, and trusts what she sees in the mirror."

**Beat 4: Now** (Resolution)
Where they are today. Grounded, specific, not hyperbolic.

"{{CLIENT_STORY_NOW}}"

> Example: "She does not call it a transformation. She calls it clarity."

### Client Story Rules
- Always frame as clarity, never as makeover
- The transformation is internal (understanding) producing external effects (better choices)
- Never reverse this order
- Use real details with permission, or anonymize completely
- {{CLIENT_STORY_RULE}}

## 5. UGC Curation

### What to Amplify
- Content that demonstrates the product in use
- Before/after comparisons (when authentic)
- Specific moments of realization or delight
- Content that aligns with brand voice naturally

### What to Skip
- Generic praise without specificity
- Content with poor visual quality
- Content that misrepresents the product
- Content that uses language contradicting brand voice

### UGC Reposting Rules
- Always credit the original creator
- Ask permission before reposting (DM first)
- Add brand context in caption without overwriting their voice
- {{UGC_REPOST_RULE}}

## 6. Social Proof Placement

### Where to Use Testimonials

| Location | Format | Length |
|----------|--------|--------|
| Landing page | Pull quote with attribution | 1-2 sentences |
| Email | Inline quote in body copy | 1 sentence |
| Social media | Full testimonial as post content | 2-4 sentences |
| Product page | Star rating + short quote | 1 sentence |
| Ad copy | Single powerful line | Under 15 words |

### Placement Rules
- Place social proof after the value proposition, not before
- Match testimonial content to the surrounding copy context
- Use testimonials that address the specific objection the reader likely has at that point
- Never stack more than 2 testimonials in sequence
- {{SOCIAL_PROOF_RULE}}
