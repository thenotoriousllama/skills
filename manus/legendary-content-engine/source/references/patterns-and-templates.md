## 1. Structural Patterns Expanded

### Pattern 1: Identity Disruption

Challenge a false belief to open space for new understanding.

**Structure**: [False belief] + [Reframe] + [New truth]

**Example 1:**
"{{IDENTITY_DISRUPTION_EXAMPLE_1}}"

> Example: "You are not bad at style. You are just wearing the wrong colors."

**Example 2:**
"{{IDENTITY_DISRUPTION_EXAMPLE_2}}"

**Example 3:**
"{{IDENTITY_DISRUPTION_EXAMPLE_3}}"

**When to use**: Headlines, ad hooks, email subject lines, social post openers.
Best when the audience holds a specific false belief about themselves.

### Pattern 2: Progressive Realization

Short lines that build meaning sequentially. The reader convinces themselves.

**Structure**: [Statement] + [Refinement] + [Refinement] + [Landing]

**Example 1:**
"{{PROGRESSIVE_REALIZATION_EXAMPLE_1}}"

> Example: "It is not the clothes. It is not the brands. It is alignment."

**Example 2:**
"{{PROGRESSIVE_REALIZATION_EXAMPLE_2}}"

**Example 3:**
"{{PROGRESSIVE_REALIZATION_EXAMPLE_3}}"

**When to use**: Email body copy, landing page sections, conversion moments.
Best when building toward a single insight.

### Pattern 3: Founder Narrative

Story-driven copy from a specific perspective with clear voice.

**Structure**: [Scene] + [Observation] + [Insight] + [Conviction]

**Example 1:**
"{{FOUNDER_NARRATIVE_EXAMPLE_1}}"

> Example: "This is her vision. My role is to make it real."

**Example 2:**
"{{FOUNDER_NARRATIVE_EXAMPLE_2}}"

**When to use**: About pages, brand stories, investor decks, long-form social.

### Pattern 4: Precision Statements

Strong declarative lines that carry weight through brevity.

**Structure**: [Short declarative sentence. Period.]

**Example 1:**
"{{PRECISION_STATEMENT_EXAMPLE_1}}"

> Example: "Clarity over cleverness. Always."

**Example 2:**
"{{PRECISION_STATEMENT_EXAMPLE_2}}"

**Example 3:**
"{{PRECISION_STATEMENT_EXAMPLE_3}}"

**When to use**: CTAs, taglines, section headers, social post closers.

### Pattern 5: Validation Through Specificity

Name the reader's experience so precisely they feel seen.

**Structure**: [Specific scenario the reader recognizes] + [Naming what they felt]

**Example 1:**
"{{VALIDATION_SPECIFICITY_EXAMPLE_1}}"

> Example: "You walk into a store, pick up something beautiful, hold it to your face, and something is off."

**Example 2:**
"{{VALIDATION_SPECIFICITY_EXAMPLE_2}}"

**Example 3:**
"{{VALIDATION_SPECIFICITY_EXAMPLE_3}}"

**When to use**: Email openers, landing page hooks, social media long-form.
Best when building trust through recognition.

### Pattern 6: Soft Reframe

Acknowledge what the reader believes, then gently redirect.

**Structure**: [Acknowledgment of current belief] + [Gentle pivot] + [New perspective]

**Example 1:**
"{{SOFT_REFRAME_EXAMPLE_1}}"

> Example: "You know what you like. But do you know what likes you back?"

**Example 2:**
"{{SOFT_REFRAME_EXAMPLE_2}}"

**Example 3:**
"{{SOFT_REFRAME_EXAMPLE_3}}"

**When to use**: Conversion copy, FAQ responses, objection handling, nurture emails.

## 2. Output Templates

### Landing Page Template (500-1000 words)

```
[HERO SECTION]
Pattern: Identity Disruption
"{{LANDING_HERO_HEADLINE}}"
Subhead: {{LANDING_HERO_SUBHEAD}}

[PROBLEM SECTION]
Pattern: Validation Through Specificity
Name the reader's experience in 2-3 short paragraphs.
{{LANDING_PROBLEM_COPY}}

[REFRAME SECTION]
Pattern: Progressive Realization
Build toward the insight that shifts how they see the problem.
{{LANDING_REFRAME_COPY}}

[SOLUTION SECTION]
Pattern: Precision Statements
Introduce {{BRAND_NAME}} as the answer. Frame as a system, not a tool.
{{LANDING_SOLUTION_COPY}}

[SOCIAL PROOF SECTION]
1-2 testimonials using the Validation Through Specificity pattern.
{{LANDING_SOCIAL_PROOF}}

[CTA SECTION]
Pattern: Soft Reframe
Soft, confident, conversational.
"{{LANDING_CTA}}"
```

### Email Template (150-400 words)

```
Subject: {{EMAIL_SUBJECT}}
(Create curiosity or recognition, never urgency)

[OPENING - 1-2 sentences]
Pattern: Validation Through Specificity
{{EMAIL_OPENING}}

[BODY - 3-5 short paragraphs]
Pattern: Progressive Realization or Soft Reframe
{{EMAIL_BODY}}

[CTA - 1 sentence]
Outcome-based, not mechanic-based.
"{{EMAIL_CTA}}"
(e.g., "See your colors" not "Click here to sign up")
```

### Ad Copy Template (1-3 lines)

```
[HOOK LINE]
Pattern: Identity Disruption
"{{AD_HOOK}}"

[SUPPORT LINE] (optional)
"{{AD_SUPPORT}}"

[CTA LINE]
"{{AD_CTA}}"
```

### Product Description Template (50-150 words)

```
[WHAT IT REVEALS]
Frame as what the user gains in understanding, not what the product does technically.
"{{PRODUCT_REVEAL}}"

[HOW IT WORKS] (1-2 sentences)
Methodology, not mechanics.
"{{PRODUCT_HOW}}"

[WHAT CHANGES]
The outcome in the reader's life.
"{{PRODUCT_OUTCOME}}"
```

## 3. Anti-Patterns

### Anti-Pattern 1: The Corporate Voice

Wrong: "Our platform leverages advanced AI to optimize your personal style choices."
Right: "{{ANTI_CORPORATE_RIGHT}}"

> Example right: "You are not bad at style. You are just wearing the wrong colors."

The fix: Remove all corporate language. Read it out loud. If it sounds like
a press release, rewrite it as something you would say to a friend.

### Anti-Pattern 2: The Hype Machine

Wrong: "Revolutionary! Game-changing! The ultimate style solution!"
Right: "{{ANTI_HYPE_RIGHT}}"

> Example right: "A system that shows you what was always yours."

The fix: Delete every superlative. Replace with specificity.

### Anti-Pattern 3: The Over-Explainer

Wrong: "What we mean by this is that the colors that naturally complement your
skin's undertone, depth, and contrast level will create a more harmonious
visual impression when worn near your face."
Right: "{{ANTI_OVEREXPLAIN_RIGHT}}"

> Example right: "The right colors make you look like yourself."

The fix: If you need more than two sentences to explain it, you do not
understand it well enough yet. Simplify, then simplify again.

### Anti-Pattern 4: The Urgency Pusher

Wrong: "Limited time offer! Only 3 spots left! Sign up before midnight!"
Right: "{{ANTI_URGENCY_RIGHT}}"

> Example right: "When you are ready, we are here."

The fix: Replace urgency with confidence. The product does not need to
pressure anyone. It needs to be clearly valuable.

### Anti-Pattern 5: The Emotional Promiser

Wrong: "You will feel confident and beautiful every single day!"
Right: "{{ANTI_EMOTIONAL_PROMISE_RIGHT}}"

> Example right: "You will understand why some things work and others do not."

The fix: Promise clarity, not feelings. Feelings are the reader's domain.

## 4. Objection Handling

### Price Objection

Never defend the price. Reframe what the price replaces.

"{{PRICE_OBJECTION_RESPONSE}}"

> Example: "Think about the last five things you bought that you never wore. That is the cost of not knowing your colors."

### Skepticism Objection ("Is this real?")

Lead with methodology, not marketing.

"{{SKEPTICISM_OBJECTION_RESPONSE}}"

> Example: "This is not a quiz. It is a five-layer analysis based on undertone, depth, contrast, clarity, and context."

### Vanity Objection ("I do not care about fashion")

Reframe away from fashion entirely.

"{{VANITY_OBJECTION_RESPONSE}}"

> Example: "This is not about fashion. It is about alignment. Knowing your colors means spending less time, less money, and less energy on things that do not work."

### Trust Objection ("Why should I trust an app?")

Anchor in expertise and methodology.

"{{TRUST_OBJECTION_RESPONSE}}"

> Example: "The methodology behind {{BRAND_NAME}} is the same one used by professional color analysts. We made it accessible."

### "I already know my colors" Objection

Validate and deepen.

"{{ALREADY_KNOW_OBJECTION_RESPONSE}}"

> Example: "If you already have a sense of your colors, you are ahead. {{BRAND_NAME}} confirms what you know and shows you what you have been missing."
